          jrp a year ago

          The real tragedy is that the code you write above is the wrong approach. In a highly concurrent system, these operations are best executed in the database, that's why you have complex update queries. Otherwise, this would just create a concurrency lock due to an overabundance of transactions.

          So, even if you have ACID properties, the sort of code above is the wrong approach. It doesn't take advantage of Atomicity. You should avoid this pattern of loading a value, incrementing it application-side and storing it back. You should simply affect the value directly with a db operation (be it a NoSQL $inc or a SQL UPDATE).

          So, if you were doing this correctly with MongoDB, you'd simply write:
          db.Account.update({_id: ..., balance: {$gte: amount}}, {$inc: { balance: amount }});

          This guarantees you can only update the account if it still has enough balance, and does so directly in the database as an atomic operation. Then you can take care of updating the other document on the receiving end.

          Granted, this will not take care of other ACID properties, but at least it's atomic and will only cause issues if the server crashes. If you were doing this rather than the application-side in-memory approach, it wouldn't be an issue.

          So, the problem is not with MongoDB. It's with programmers not understanding the tools they're using, and the culture of rockstar programming which encourages rapid-application-development with poorly written ODMs/ORMs and the ensuing technical debt.

              Robert Escriva Mod jrp a year ago

              MongoDB's update operation will only get you so far. Assuming it's correct (a whole other discussion entirely), the atomic update will only affect one document. It's still possible for one operation or the other to fail, leading to lost updates and irreconcilable account balances.

              HyperDex also provides atomic update operations (http://hyperdex.org/doc/latest... which we omitted from this article for clarity. You could update the account with "db.cond_atomic_sub('accounts', 'src', {'balance': GreaterEqual(amount)}, {'balance': amount})" and "db.atomic_add('accounts', 'dst', {'balance': amount})".

              Now take those two operations, roll them up in a HyperDex Warp transaction, and you're guaranteed to always transfer the money from one account to the other without any possibility for inconsistency. The transaction guarantees both updates happen, or the pair fail without effect.

              The problem is not with programmers' understanding. There's a pervasive and wrong movement that encourages people to use eventually consistent systems. They will justify it with statements of the form, "If it's good enough for banks, then it's good enough for you." This thinking is dangerous.

              At the end of the day, your application is the sum of its invariants. If the data store underneath your system is incapable of providing you with guarantees---or worse, requires extensive care, and a $100K support contract to provide you with guarantees---your application is broken and blaming the developers for this is a cop-out. We should be holding database vendors (especially those with hundreds of million in shiny VC cash) to a higher standard.

                                  Ryan Berryman a year ago

                                  Great article highlighting the risks in new financial intermediaries. Banks do not (and can not) operate the way you describe, however. In the Cap theorem, banks usually opt for eventual consistency because they need partition tolerance (else: unrecoverable corrupted accounting) and accessibility (else: bank not available = run on the bank). This leads banks to be vulnerable to the attack you describe. One example (similar to the attack you write about) is check kiting. Banks guard against these attacks (and others) using specific fraud prevention controls (likely using out of band checks on number of transactions, transaction amounts, and other pattern recognition methods - making different CAP tradeoff selections for those systems). I think this example is just one area where the IP related to fraud built up within financial institutions over centuries has not been fully encapsulated by those in the crypto-currency world.

                                      tefan Oravec a year ago

                                      What you actually need to avoid here is lost update, which has to be handled in almost any DB the same way - either using serializable isolation level or using locking. Which can be either pesimistic - lock - update - unlock or optimistic - begin - update - validate - commit/rollback & reset/merge/retry. Same as with let's say SVN with locking vs. SVN without locking. First one is oldschool approach used in Client/Server statefulll session applications, which is safe, but inhibits the concurrency and brings overhead with locks management. What we do in web/distributed, highly concurrent stateless apps is the second approach - we are optimistic, that only we are updating data and deal with conflict when it arise. Upon update you check that you update the same data, that you've read - you read some record version number/system change number/compute checksum of read data and than use it to check, that it did't change when you update it. When it is not the same, you deal with it - either inform user, that other user has changed the data, or you reread data and restart/retry.

                                                                                                                        Emin Gun Sirer Mod Philip Levis a year ago

                                                                                                                        The bank/ATM example makes perfect sense simply because Flexcoin was a bank. Reality is telling us something here, and it's goes against the dogma that you often hear repeated.

                                                                                                                        The oft-repeated statement that "banks are not ACID" misses the point. Of course banks are "not ACID": any system that allows you to back-date a transaction is not linearizable.

                                                                                                                        A bank timeline is delta-consistent and monotonic. That is, the interface posed by the bank to the outside world typically never goes backwards, and there is a time period delta after which the timeline quiesces.

                                                                                                                        The thing is, you cannot build delta-consistency or monotonicity on top of a data store that is just eventually-consistent. And in fact, banks don't. They use ACID databases to record their internal processes. That's why when you make a transaction and call later to see if the same system still has the data you recorded, they can give you a definitive answer. People would revolt if their deposits appeared in their account, then disappeared, then reappeared. A bank, which is an amalgamation of many systems, might appear "eventually consistent" to a casual observer (though it is typically delta-consistent and monotonic), but it's internal architecture relies on ACID systems.

                                                                                                                        The "banks are not ACID" meme got pushed hard by people trying to defend eventual consistency. Find me a bank that maintains its accounting on a first-gen NoSQL database and I'll show you a broken bank.

                                                                                                                                      The confusion here is stemming from the SLA at the bank level, which is eventually consistent (actually, it's delta-consistent, but for this discussion, we can be imprecise to favor the EC argument), versus how the bank operates internally, which is typically ACID. Yes, it's absolutely true that the banking system overall is "eventually consistent." But this doesn't mean that individual components of this system can behave in an EC manner with impunity. I'm not aware of any regulated banks that employ anything other than ACID databases for maintaining customer accounts and branch operations. Are you? Even customer support hotline calls are typically kept in ACID databases even though they play absolutely no role in a bank's externally visible SLA, because it is critical from a business perspective that they behave monotonically ("non-erratically" is what a customer would call it if she called and her previous calls were not recorded).
